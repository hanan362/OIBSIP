The Enchantress of Numbers

Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, was born in London in 1815, the only legitimate child of the poet Lord Byron. Her mother, wary of the instability she associated with poetry, steered Ada firmly toward mathematics and logic from an early age — a curriculum unusual for a woman of her time, and one that would shape the rest of her life.

In 1833, at just seventeen, Ada met Charles Babbage, the inventor of the Difference Engine and, later, the far more ambitious Analytical Engine. Babbage's machines were mechanical forerunners of the modern computer, and Ada was one of the very few people of her era who grasped not just how they worked, but what they might eventually mean.

Between 1842 and 1843, Ada translated an Italian paper on the Analytical Engine and appended her own notes — notes that ran three times longer than the original text. Buried in "Note G" was an algorithm to compute Bernoulli numbers using the Engine: a sequence of operations designed to be carried out by a machine. Many historians consider it the first published computer program.

More striking still was her insight that the Engine's operations weren't limited to numbers. If a symbol could represent a relationship, Ada reasoned, the machine could manipulate anything expressible in that form — music, images, language. She had described the general-purpose computer more than a century before one was built.

A Life in Milestones

  1. 1815

    Born in London

    Daughter of Lord Byron and Annabella Milbanke. Her parents separated within weeks of her birth, and she never met her father.

  2. 1833

    Meets Charles Babbage

    Introduced at a party, she is shown a working prototype of his Difference Engine and is immediately captivated.

  3. 1835

    Marries William King

    She becomes Countess of Lovelace when her husband is made an Earl in 1838.

  4. 1843

    Publishes her Notes

    Her translation and annotations on Babbage's Analytical Engine are published, including what is now recognised as the first algorithm intended for machine execution.

  5. 1852

    Dies at 36

    She passes away from uterine cancer, the same age at which her father died, and is buried beside him at his request.

  6. 1980

    Ada programming language

    The U.S. Department of Defense names a newly standardised programming language "Ada" in her honour.

Why She Still Matters

Ada Lovelace's notes went largely unnoticed in her own lifetime — the Analytical Engine was never completed, and her ideas waited nearly a century for hardware to catch up. But when computing did arrive, her central intuition proved exactly right: a machine that manipulates symbols according to rules isn't limited to arithmetic. It can be general-purpose. It can be creative.

Every October, Ada Lovelace Day celebrates women in STEM in her name — a fitting tribute to someone who saw further into the future of computing than almost anyone else alive at the time.
